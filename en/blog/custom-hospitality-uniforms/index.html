Custom Hospitality Uniforms: Designing Durable Workwear for Hotel Staff

March 10, 2026 • Updated: March 10, 2026 • By Leonardo Gobbato

In a project at METT Barcelona, a newly opened 5-star luxury hotel, our studio created a complete staff uniform system from the ground up. We began with the hotel’s architectural renders and interior design concept for the bar, restaurant, and spa. Uniforms were tailored to the hotel’s color palette and material finishes so the clothing feels like part of the environment.

The F&B manager requested distinct outfits for more than 20 team members, plus managers, each specific to their role. We developed bespoke garments for front-of-house, back-of-house, and management. Fabrics were chosen for performance, mostly organic cotton blended with technical fibers to ensure comfort, easy care, and longevity. All pieces were designed, sampled, and produced in Barcelona (315 items in total) using local textiles and manufacturing.

After one year of daily service, uniforms are still in excellent condition and staff report no complaints. In hospitality, that usually means the clothing is working exactly as intended.

Aligning Uniform Design with Hotel Concept

We treated every garment as an extension of the hotel’s brand and architecture. Uniforms are often the first point of contact with guests, so they must communicate tone, character, and service positioning at a glance.

Our designs took direct inspiration from METT’s spaces. Spa teams wear uniforms in subtle green drawn from the décor, while restaurant and bar staff wear warm neutrals and deep blues reflecting interior finishes. We collaborated with the interior design team to align trims and accents. For example, copper details from the bar area became trim on jackets.

Premium fabrics and fade-resistant treatments were selected so uniforms keep their appearance under heavy use. The result is a coherent visual system where each role aligns with the architecture while preserving one unified brand language.

Selecting Durable, Comfortable Fabrics

Hospitality uniforms face intensive wear and frequent industrial laundering. In real operations, garments may be washed at very high temperatures with industrial detergents. To handle this, we specified industrial-laundry-grade textiles.

We used blends of organic cotton with polyester or elastane. Cotton offers breathability and softness, while technical fibers add strength, stretch, wrinkle control, and shape retention. All fabrics were mechanically pre-shrunk and dyed with high-quality pigments to resist fading.

We also applied practical finishes. Some aprons and chef coats received stain-resistant and water-repellent treatments, and high-stress areas such as knees and elbows were double-stitched. Across repeated wash-cycle testing, fabrics maintained color and structure consistently.

Differentiating Uniforms by Role

A key project decision was defining a wardrobe for each staff category. Front-of-house roles (reception, servers, concierge) received smart, tailored pieces that project professionalism while supporting long shifts. Servers and bartenders wore fitted shirts, vests, and jackets in soft-stretch fabrics for better mobility.

Back-of-house teams (chefs, kitchen assistants, housekeeping) received rugged and functional uniforms. Chef jackets and pants were cut for active work, aprons were selected for spill-prone tasks, and housekeeping uniforms prioritized breathable comfort.

Managers received bespoke linen or cotton suits in neutral tones aligned with the hotel’s luxury positioning. This visual differentiation through color, fabric, and cut helps teams identify roles quickly and helps guests understand who does what.

Local Production and Sustainability

All development stages took place locally in Barcelona, including fabric sourcing. Keeping design and production close to home improved communication, quality control, and lead time.

It also supported sustainability goals. Local production reduces transport impact and strengthens ethical sourcing visibility. We prioritized natural and renewable fibers, especially linen and organic cotton, whenever operational requirements allowed.

Durable materials designed for frequent washing also reduce waste by extending garment life. This project combined local manufacturing with practical sustainability choices that support both the hotel’s environmental direction and day-to-day operational efficiency.

Maintenance, Performance, and Staff Feedback

After one year of daily use, we evaluated the garments in detail. Nearly every piece remained crisp, colorfast, and structurally stable. High-wear zones such as collars, cuffs, and pockets showed minimal fraying despite repeated industrial washing.

Staff reported that uniforms remained comfortable and easy to maintain. In practice, well-fitting, comfortable uniforms support morale and reduce friction in service execution. The METT team reported no recurring fit or function issues.

In hospitality operations, the absence of uniform complaints is usually one of the strongest indicators that the clothing system is doing its job.

Conclusion

The METT Barcelona program shows that hotel uniforms perform best when treated as integrated systems, not isolated garments. By aligning design with hotel identity, selecting fabrics built for heavy use, tailoring each wardrobe to real roles, and planning for simple maintenance, uniforms can support both daily operations and brand consistency.

For hospitality teams, the practical takeaway is clear: invest early in fabric quality and role-specific design logic. The return appears in durability, team confidence, and guest perception over time. You can also review our project process and selected projects for related operational examples.
